THE PROBLEM
Why this exists

Every financial decision involves uncertainty about the future, but most analysis still leans on a single point estimate — one expected return, one assumed volatility, one number you put in a slide. That number lies. It hides the entire shape of what could actually happen.

Monte Carlo simulation answers a different question: not "what will happen," but "what's the distribution of things that could happen?" Run ten thousand possible futures, look at where they cluster, where the worst ones land, what the median outcome looks like.

This tool is built for that kind of thinking. It runs real geometric Brownian motion paths in your browser, then lets you walk around the result in 3D — every dot is one possible future for the portfolio you defined.

THE APPROACH
How it works

Geometric Brownian Motion

The simulation models stock price evolution using the standard GBM stochastic differential equation. Each day's return is drawn from a normal distribution centered on the daily drift, scaled by the daily volatility — adjusted for the continuous-time correction term that keeps the math honest:

St+1 = St · exp[(μ − σ²/2)Δt + σ · Z√Δt]

Where μ is the expected annual return, σ is the annual volatility, Δt is the time step (1/252 for daily), and Z is a standard normal random draw. The Box-Muller transform converts uniform random numbers into the normally distributed shocks we need.

What gets simulated

For each of the simulations you request, the engine generates a complete daily price path over the time horizon — typically 252 days for one year. Each path starts at the current price and ends somewhere else. Where it ends defines the terminal return, which is what the analytics aggregate: percentiles, mean, standard deviation, Value at Risk.

Three-dimensional visualization

Most Monte Carlo tools dump output into a 2D scatter plot or histogram. This one renders every simulated path as a line in 3D space — X axis is time, Y axis is price, Z axis is which simulation it belongs to. Drag to rotate. The cluster shape itself tells you something a histogram can't: how outcomes diverge over time, where the fat tails are, how the cone of uncertainty opens up.

TAKEAWAYS
What this taught me
01
VOLATILITY IS NOT RISK
Standard deviation tells you how much prices wiggle. Value at Risk tells you what happens at the bad end of the distribution. They're related but not the same — and the difference matters for any decision involving real money.
02
FAT TAILS ARE EVERYWHERE
GBM assumes returns are log-normal, which is a lie that's mostly fine. Real markets crash harder and rally harder than GBM predicts. The next iteration of this tool would let you swap the distribution — Student's t-distribution for fatter tails, or jump-diffusion for crash modeling.
03
RUNTIME IS A FEATURE
Running 10,000 paths × 252 days is 2.5 million random draws. Doing that in the browser in under 250ms required vectorized Box-Muller and avoiding allocations in the inner loop. Performance work is product work.
04
3D CHANGES UNDERSTANDING
A 2D fan chart shows the cone of uncertainty. A 3D path cloud shows the texture inside it — clusters, outliers, the visual difference between low-vol and high-vol regimes. Pictures matter when you're trying to communicate risk to someone who doesn't read distribution functions.
